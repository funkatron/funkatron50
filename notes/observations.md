# Observations

Working notes about patterns across Funkatron 50.

These are hypotheses, not conclusions. Keep them loose until several song dossiers exist.

## Early observations

### Genre labels are not the main structure

The playlist crosses industrial, metal, hip-hop, electro, alternative rock, new wave, and pop, but the more meaningful structure may be about musical function: rhythm, texture, machinery, production, atmosphere, memory, and impact.

### Production often seems central

Many tracks are built around production as an expressive force: samples, drum machines, distortion, loops, remix culture, dense mixes, and machine-like rhythm.

### Rhythm is a major connective tissue

Helmet, Prong, Ministry, Public Enemy, Gap Band, Beastie Boys, Front 242, Underworld, and The Prodigy all point toward rhythm as a primary attraction, even when the genres differ.

### Heavy music and electronic music overlap constantly

The collection repeatedly blurs the boundary between guitars and machines: Ministry, Killing Joke, Nine Inch Nails, PIG, Cyber-Tec, Front 242, Chemlab, Spineshank, Prong, and The Damning Well.

### Several choices are non-obvious artist selections

This matters because the project is personal, not canonical. Examples:

- Iron Maiden: `Losfer Words`, not the more obvious singles.
- Midnight Oil: `Bullroarer`, not `Beds Are Burning`.
- Nine Inch Nails: `Last` and `That's What I Get`, not only the largest cultural hits.
- Public Enemy: `She Watch Channel Zero?!`, linking hip-hop, sampling, and heavy guitar.

## Threads to track

- Machines and bodies
- Rhythm as architecture
- Industrial culture
- Sampling and collage
- Heavy guitars as texture

- Gateway tracks
